## Analytical Framework

All tools below were developed by the user. Apply them when evaluating, comparing, or visualizing games.

### 1. Immersion Equation

All variables scored **1–5 (integers only)**:

| Variable | Name | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **M** | Mechanical Depth | Complexity and interlocking systems |
| **T** | Fiction-Mechanic Embodiment | How much mechanics *generate* (not just reference) the thematic experience |
| **G** | Meaningful Agency | Player decisions that matter and shape outcomes |
| **F** | Friction | Setup time, fiddliness, rules overhead, barriers to play |
| **Ar** | Art/Production Quality | Visual design, component quality, table presence |

**Game Score (GS)** = (M × T × G) − F

**Immersion Score (IS)** = GS × (Ar ÷ 2)

#### Key Principles

- **T Gatekeeper Principle:** T is multiplicative and acts as a score floor. Low T collapses the entire IS regardless of M or G quality. This is the user's non-negotiable variable.
- **Fiction-mechanic embodiment vs. decoration:** A mechanic scores high T not by *referencing* a theme but by *generating* the thematic experience. Example: Sky Team's silence rule doesn't describe cockpit pressure — it creates it.
- **IS paths can differ:** Games can reach similar IS scores via different variable profiles (e.g., higher T vs. higher Ar). The model distinguishes these as different experiential signatures.
- **Ar filters real preferences:** Ar legitimately excludes games the user wouldn't enjoy regardless of mechanical quality (e.g., Too Many Bones excluded due to Gearloc art style).

#### Solo-First Scoring Lens

When scoring games for solo play, adjust:
- **T downward** when solo mode structurally reduces thematic tension (e.g., area control games using an automa instead of real opponents)
- **G downward** when solo mode imposes automa-driven constraints that reduce meaningful player agency

## User Profile Summary

- **Solo-first narrativist** — solo play is the primary mode
- **Horror-atmospheric core** — Lovecraft/sci-fi horror is the preferred thematic territory
- **Co-op over competitive** (~3:1 ratio)
- **Early in his board gaming journey** — still developing the habit of regular play. Discovered narrative campaign games like Arkham Horror LCG and Gloomhaven: Jaws of the Lion and was drawn in by the immersion and the ability to experience long adventures across self-contained sessions. This shaped his taste toward deep, story-driven games.
- **Chasing emotional architecture over points** — theme-mechanic integration matters more than optimization puzzles
- **Hard filter:** Games requiring miniature painting raise F significantly and are excluded. Pre-assembled finished figurines or ready beautiful figurines are acceptable.
- **Art filter:** Dislikes goblin-with-long-ears aesthetic (Too Many Bones). Prefers character design in the style of Avatar, Saga comics, or artists from games such as On Mars, Galactic Cruise, Nemesis, The Bloody Inn, Imperium: Horizons, Cyberion, Arkham Horror, Sky Team, Burgle Bros, Harmonies, and Endeavor: Deep Sea, Spirit Island.

**\*Nemesis note:** The miniatures came unpainted and required commissioning someone to paint them. This significantly raised F (from what would otherwise be 3 to 4) and lowered Ar (from a potential 5 to 4, since the out-of-box experience wasn't finished). Nemesis is the case study for the user's hard filter against unpainted miniatures — he loves the game but the friction cost was high enough to establish "no more games with large quantities of unfinished figurines" as a permanent rule.

**Model validation:** All "Loves it" games score IS ≥ 87.5, while "Likes it" games fall in the 34–39 range. The Bloody Inn and Sky Team converge at identical IS (87.5) via different experiential paths — Sky Team through silence-as-mechanic embodiment, The Bloody Inn through dark art direction elevating macabre card mechanics.
